# What I did while you were at work — 10 June

All done and committed locally (nothing pushed, no PRs opened). Three commits, one per batch. Everything was tested and I ran real 1:1s through the engine to watch each change actually work.

## The short version

The engine stops doing four things it shouldn't, and starts showing you two things it didn't before. None of it is a rewrite — these are surgical changes to the parts we talked about.

---

## Batch 1 — honesty fixes (mostly under the hood)

**1. Strong answers no longer get thrown away.**
Each question has a preset "how much can this move the score" limit. When someone gave an answer stronger than that limit allowed, the extra signal used to vanish. Now it's kept (just the numbers — never the person's words, for privacy) and handed to the final briefing, which can say *"a stronger signal came up here than the score shows — worth confirming."* The score itself still stays disciplined.

**2. Sero stops faking a score.**
Mid-meeting, if an area like wellbeing hadn't come up by question 4, the old code quietly stamped a "wellbeing" label onto an unrelated question — inventing a reading that no question earned. Now it brings in a **real** wellbeing question instead (from the queue or the question bank), and only one that fits the meeting type. If there genuinely isn't a good one, it leaves it alone and says so rather than faking it.

**3. The "retry logic" and "handoff" questions are gone.**
Two odd fallback questions left over from an old test could pop up in any session. Replaced with neutral ones ("What did you expect would happen there?") that fit any conversation.

**4. Empty-note guessing removed.**
If you didn't write a note and didn't pick a focus, the old code guessed from keywords — so "growth of the codebase" wrongly triggered a "Growth" focus. That guessing is gone. It now falls back to a note-driven topic if there is one, otherwise the first listed, and it tells you it fell back instead of pretending.

**5. Ticking 2–3 focus areas now actually does something.**
Before, only the *first* topic you ticked shaped the brief and questions. Now up to three all flow through (the first stays the main one). Capped at three on purpose — more than that and the question generator spreads too thin.

**6. Two briefing promises are now checked.**
The briefing is supposed to (a) acknowledge whatever the employee asked to cover, and (b) suggest a re-run when the session was too thin. Nothing enforced that before. Now there's a check — a gentle warning in normal use, and a hard test failure if a future change breaks it.

## Batch 2 — you'll see this on the prep screens

- **Each focus area card** gets a small grey tag: *"from your note"* vs *"common for this level"* — so you know whether Sero is reacting to what you told it or just to the role.
- **The prep brief** now has two new lines: **"How sure is this"** (e.g. *"Medium — based on your note and her seniority"*) and **"Don't assume yet"** (the one conclusion the data does *not* yet support). This is the piece that stops you walking in with a verdict already formed.

## Batch 3 — you'll see this in the final briefing

A new short section: **"How engaged they seem"**. Four honest levels — *inconclusive*, *no clear concern*, *worth checking*, *clear concern* — each with the evidence behind it, what's still missing, one move you control, and what to watch next time.

Two safety rails:
- It **never** prints flat labels like "disengaged" or "flight risk" as a verdict (it can still quote the employee saying something like that — that's evidence, not a label).
- If the session was thin, the level is **forced** to "inconclusive" no matter what — the model is never trusted to call disengagement off weak data.
