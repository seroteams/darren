The problem

Most 1:1s are improvised. Most "AI for managers" makes that worse.

Generic tools hand you a list of generic questions and a cheerful summary that sounds insightful whether or not anything was actually said. A manager doesn't need more confident-sounding text. They need to walk in prepared, ask the right thing, and walk out knowing what they actually learned — including "not much, this one was thin."

That last part — admitting a weak read instead of faking a strong one — is the whole engineering problem. It's where we've spent this cycle.

The hard part

The interesting work isn't generating text. It's refusing to.

A model will happily invent a premise, reuse a private note, score a vague "fine" as good news, or sound certain about one offhand comment. This cycle we built guardrails for exactly those failures — each one a named check that fails the build if it regresses.

CROSS_SESSION_QUESTION_LEAK

Questions stay in their own run

Anything the engine invents mid-meeting saves to an isolated pool — it can never resurface in an unrelated person's 1:1.

UNGROUNDED_PREMISE

No invented backstory

Every new question must quote real words from the notes or transcript — or be marked open. Made-up premises ("you're up for promotion") get dropped, not reworded.

QUESTION_ARC_LEAK

A check-in stays a check-in

Casual and "something feels off" meetings can't smuggle in performance-judging questions. Protection now sits right where questions are chosen.

honest thread-follow

"You said…" actually quotes you

Follow-ups lift a real, contiguous phrase from the answer. No clean phrase available? The follow-up is skipped — no more garbled stems.

AXIS_SILENT_SESSION

Real talk gets scored

A substantive conversation can't ship with every axis marked "didn't come up" — carried-forward questions keep their scoring power.

RULE_ECHO_MEANING

Confidence has to be earned

A high score resting on one or two mentions gets its confidence downgraded — and answers that just echo a prompt example are flagged, never silently rewritten.

The rule under all of them: say what's actually true. Don't borrow questions, don't invent backstory, don't fake confidence — and never paper over a problem to look polished.

Keeping ourselves honest

We reviewed 48 of our own runs and graded every one.

A product about honest reads has to hold itself to the same bar. So we sat down with a batch of real pipeline runs and gave each a blunt verdict — keep it, fix it, or block it — then turned every "fix" into engine work.

27 Keep
20 Fix
1 Block

Those 20 fixes are exactly where this cycle's trust checks came from. The review isn't marketing — the verdicts live in the run logs, and the fixes are locked behind gates so they can't quietly come back.

How we build

Small steps. Tested by a human. Then committed.

Every meaningful change is split into phases, each ending in real test scenarios. One phase at a time. The product owner walks the scenarios and green-lights before the next phase starts — the builder never marks its own homework.

Question pool
Session-isolated. Runtime questions split out so nothing leaks between people.
Thread-follow
Honest stems. Follow-ups quote the real answer or step aside.
Planner
Grounding gate. Every new question cites real words from the session.
Relational arc
Guarded at the question layer. Check-ins can't drift into assessment.
Axes
Survive the planner. Real conversations get a real score.
Briefing
Confidence honesty. Thin evidence means a downgraded read.

One board tracks all of it — what's in flight, what's parked, what we cut. Cut scope stays cut.

What's next

Hardening, then continuity.

The near-term roadmap is unglamorous on purpose: tighten the contracts between stages, add a safe fallback if a briefing ever fails, and build session continuity — save and resume a run, and see past sessions for context.
