What Was Audited

The machine under the hood.

A manager types sparse notes about a direct report. Sero returns a preparation briefing, runs an adaptive live question loop, and closes with a scored, two-channel honest read. Five model stages, one weak link.

01Focus pointsPicks the meeting's themes, filtered by relationship arc
02PreparationThe pre-meeting briefing: core issue, opening question, what to avoid
03Question bankCandidate questions following the meeting type's arc
04Live turn loopRe-plans every turn against the axis state — scored 55–65/100 on thread-following vs 85–98 elsewhere
05EvaluationFinal briefing: axes, two "brutal truth" channels, next actions

Stage 04 dominates cost too: ~$0.33 of the $0.36 per run. It is both the quality problem and the cost lever — the fix track is already in flight (docs/plans/doing/thread-follow).

The Panel

Six verdicts.

A simulated committee: each verdict is written in the voice of that person's public thinking, applied to today's audit evidence. None of them has actually seen Sero.

GT
Gary TanPresident & CEO, Y Combinator — presiding

"This is one of the strongest pre-user codebases I've seen — and that's exactly the problem. You've spent months getting brilliant at the half of the startup that was never in doubt. Make something people want has two clauses. You've nailed 'make something'. You have zero evidence on 'people want'. The good news: you are literally three conversations away from starting to find out, and that costs nothing."

What impressed him

Founder can clearly build and ship alone at startup-team speed; the honesty engineering is a genuine product philosophy, not marketing.

What worried him

The build-trap signature: engineering tracks keep closing while the one task that produces evidence — naming three test managers — sits untouched.

📋 His call: Stop building for two weeks. Put the product in front of three real managers before writing another feature.
AK
Andrej KarpathyAI engineering lens

"Deterministic gates that outrank the LLM judge, adversarial sentinels the baseline refuses to bless, free offline replay — this is real eval engineering. But eight golden cases is not an oracle, and your flagship behaviour is the one thing your own harness says is weak."

Praise

The gate architecture is the right shape: code decides, the model advises. Per-run cost ledgers mean unit economics are measured, not guessed.

Concern

Single-provider lock-in with no fallback path; prompt-to-regex coupling that rots silently; the turn planner is 90% of run cost and the weakest stage.

📋 His call: Finish the thread-follow fix, then grow the golden set from real user runs — that's the only oracle that matters.
DH
David Heinemeier HanssonPragmatic developer lens

"A hand-rolled server, no framework bloat, strict types that actually pass, tests that actually run — I love this. But auto-deploying main to production with no CI is driving without the seatbelt you already own. It's one YAML file. Write it today."

Praise

The majestic monolith done right: controller → service → repo, engine cleanly separated, documentation good enough to onboard a stranger.

Concern

700-line vanilla-JS god-files in the frontend; single-instance rate limiting that dies at the first horizontal scale; bleeding-edge Node pin.

📋 His call: One CI workflow (typecheck + tests before deploy), pay the $7 hosting tier, then go get customers — the code is ready enough.
JZ
Julie ZhuoDesign & management lens

"The briefing that says 'not enough signal to read' instead of faking a score — that's design integrity most teams never reach. But a manager who re-reads their briefing gets a visibly downgraded version of what they first saw. The second impression is the product."

Praise

Next actions grouped by time horizon, shareable vs private clearly badged, copy rules enforced by tests. Honest, scannable, humane.

Concern

Three parallel briefing renderers guarantee drift; no guided first-run for a new manager; an 8-stage wizard whose own escape hatch admits it's long.

📋 Her call: Unify the briefing renderer into one component, and watch three real first-runs before touching anything else.
CJ
Claire Hughes JohnsonExecutive & operations lens (ex-COO, Stripe)

"A company that cannot take money and cannot sign terms isn't a company yet — it's a project. The sequence is fixed: named testers, then a pricing hypothesis, then a paid pilot with a one-page agreement. Don't build billing before someone has said yes to a price."

Praise

The validation plan's pass bar is exactly right: a second unprompted prep within two weeks. That's a real behavioural metric, not a vanity one.

Concern

Sales, support, compliance and data deletion all route to one founder's inbox. Sensitive HR data with no terms of service is a deal-blocker for any real buyer.

📋 Her call: A one-page ToS + privacy policy before the first external tester; a price on the table by the first pilot conversation.
KS
Kim ScottFeedback & 1:1 domain lens (Radical Candor)

"Two truth channels — one the employee can see, one only for the manager — with inference banned unless the person said it themselves? That's radical candor operationalised. But thread-following is what caring personally sounds like. If the tool drops someone's answer at turn six, it fails the humanity test it set for itself."

Praise

The no-inference rule and evidence anchoring protect the employee from the manager's untested stories — rare and genuinely valuable.

Concern

Employees are the subjects of this data but not yet its stakeholders; a works council or HR counsel will ask what's said about people and where it goes.

📋 Her call: Ship the thread-follow fix before any real 1:1 runs through it, and write the employee-facing data story now.
Committee Verdict

Not yet — and it's fixable in 90 days.

"We don't fund evidence of effort. We fund evidence of desire. Come back with three managers who came back."

The committee's read: Sero is a fundable product attached to an unfundable evidence base. Nothing on the critical path is an engineering problem. The product is live, honest by construction, and better built than most funded seed companies. What's missing is one behavioural fact — does a real manager return unprompted to prep a second 1:1? Until that fact exists, every additional feature is deferred proof. The moment it exists, everything else on this page becomes a straightforward to-do list.

Risk Register

What can kill it.

Highest Demand risk — the build trap

Months of high-quality supply-side work, zero customer contact. The pass bar is written down but has never met a real manager. Every week of building without testers deepens the sunk cost.

High Data sensitivity without legal cover

Candid notes about named employees flow to a third-party model with no terms, no DPA, no formal policy. Defensible for consenting friends; an acute liability at the first paying customer.

Medium Bus factor of one

One person is the engineer, product owner, support desk, compliance officer and deletion endpoint. The exceptional docs are real mitigation — but no second human has ever touched the system.

Medium Single model provider

Every call goes to one API with no implemented fallback. A pricing change, model deprecation or outage upstream is a product outage downstream.
