The lesson

A manager gives us input in two very different places

This is the thing to hold onto. The opening note is thought-out and written before the meeting. The in-session answers are fast, clipped, spoken-aloud. They read nothing alike — and the engine has to handle both.

📝1 · The opening notebefore the meeting
"Priya shipped a major feature on Monday and the team noticed, but she's been quieter this week. I want to read whether this is normal post-launch recovery or something unresolved."
Composed, full sentences, ~40–90 words. Often carries an observation and a worry. This is where the manager sets the agenda.
💬2 · The in-session answerduring the meeting
"raises things if it'll actually change a decision. lately the timing hasn't felt there"
Short, lowercase, fragmentary — ~12 words. Typed while talking. The engine reads these to move its axes and pick the next question.

The anatomy of a good prep note

Our best freeform notes almost always have the same three parts. Once you see it, you can't unsee it — and it's exactly what the engine keys off.

Priya shipped a major feature on Monday and the team noticed, but she has been quieter this week. I want to check whether this is normal post-launch recovery or something unresolved. She also mentioned wanting to mentor a few months ago and that thread never moved. I need a clearer read on what she expects to own next quarter before I introduce the billing rewrite.

Observation — what changed, concretely Tension — the thing they're unsure about Intent — what they want out of the meeting

Inside the meeting

How answers actually come in

846 answers, and they're short — a median of 12 words. Managers type the way they'd talk. And a real slice are near-empty ("fine", "dunno") — the engine has to do something useful even when the human gives it almost nothing.

The typical answer

Median band — clipped, lowercase, real.

"not deep enough. covered the product well, didn't pin down their real pain or timeline"
"wants research in earlier, before the direction's set, so it can actually shape things"
"take the lead on vendor onboarding, pair him with someone on reporting to build that up"

The low-effort tail

The shortest real answers we logged. The hard case for the engine.

"ok""no""fine" "same""dunno""i guess"

Only 2.8% of questions were skipped outright — so managers do answer. But "answered" and "gave us something to work with" aren't the same thing.

So what

What this teaches us

1

It's our test data, not the market's

Every insight here describes what we feed the engine. The single most valuable version of this report is the one we run after the first 20 real managers — that's when the patterns mean something about willingness to pay.

2

We've over-tuned the bi-weekly

109 of 169 runs are one meeting type. Performance, growth, and "feels off" are thin. If we believe those are the emotionally sharper moments, the test corpus should grow there.

3

Good notes have a shape

Observation → tension → intent. Real managers won't write this cleanly. A prompt hint or a light input template could nudge them toward the parts the engine actually uses.

4

Plan for the one-word answer

"fine / dunno / same" is a first-class input, not an error. It's worth a deliberate check that the engine stays useful on the low-effort tail — that's the honest real-world case.
